Evolving Definitions in Computer Science

Framing the Problem

Are computer science definitions fixed truths, or are they negotiated artifacts that shift as technical practice changes? This lecture takes the position that most core definitions are stable in intent yet fluid in operational meaning.

We will examine this through concept drift across eras: from early procedural software to object-oriented abstraction, from local infrastructure to cloud-native systems, and from deterministic programs to probabilistic AI pipelines.

Guiding thesis: Definitions in CS are less like static dictionary entries and more like contracts updated by tooling, scale, and socio-technical constraints.

Historical Vocabulary Shift: Subroutine -> Function -> Method

In early programming, subroutine emphasized reusable instruction blocks and control transfer. Its center of gravity was execution mechanics: jump, perform, return.

As languages matured, function foregrounded input-output mapping, composability, and referential semantics. Later, method embedded behavior inside objects, reframing reuse as collaboration among abstractions rather than mere code sharing.

The behavior existed in primitive form before the language shifted, but the definition expanded with new expectations: modularity, testability, and design intent became part of what the term implied.

Cloud Computing and Definition Drift

Consider the term scalability. Historically, it often meant vertical hardware growth or algorithmic efficiency. In cloud-native environments, scalability now includes elasticity, autoscaling policy, failure domains, and cost-performance optimization under dynamic load.

Likewise, availability no longer means simply “the server is running.” It is negotiated through SLOs, multi-region replication, circuit breakers, chaos testing, and incident response maturity.

AI Systems and Redefining Core Ideas

The concept of program correctness illustrates a major shift. In classical software, correctness meant satisfying explicit logical specifications. In modern AI pipelines, correctness often includes statistical reliability, calibration, fairness constraints, drift tolerance, and monitoring over time.

In other words, the unit of meaning changed from a single deterministic artifact to an evolving socio-technical system: data, model, serving layer, feedback loops, and governance.

What Actually Remains Constant?

Some invariants endure, but at a high level:

  • Abstraction: hiding complexity behind stable interfaces.
  • Composition: building larger behavior from smaller units.
  • Trade-offs: performance, correctness, cost, and maintainability are always in tension.
  • Validation: claims still require evidence, even if evidence forms have changed.

These constants are methodological, not lexical. Terminology evolves because practice evolves.

Critical Reflection (Graduate-Level Position)

No concept in computer science is completely frozen in operational meaning. Even apparently stable words like algorithm, optimization, or automation are reinterpreted by hardware realities, organizational scale, regulation, and user impact. What remains constant is the underlying intellectual project: formalizing processes, managing complexity, and producing reliable outcomes under constraints.

Therefore, definitions should be treated as historically situated tools. Their value lies not in permanence but in explanatory power for current systems. A strong practitioner recognizes both semantic continuity and contextual drift, using each term with awareness of its lineage and modern implications.
